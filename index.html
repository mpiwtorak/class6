Qualifying to Ride
  • Be at least 16 years old
  • Hold a valid Alberta driver's licence (any class)
  • Have a supervisor at all times until Class 6 road test is passed
Supervisor Requirements
  • Must hold a valid FULL Class 6 licence — not GDL
  • Must provide supervision by: sitting on your motorcycle, following on another motorcycle, or following in a vehicle
⚠ Exam trap: A Class 5-GDL rider CANNOT supervise a learner. Only a full, non-GDL Class 6 holder qualifies.
Class 7 Learner Restrictions HOT
  • NO night riding — midnight or 1hr after sunset → 5am or 1hr before sunrise
  • Zero BAC AND zero drug level while seated on or riding
  • Fewer than 8 demerit points
  • Must have supervising rider at all times
Class 5-GDL Restrictions (when on a motorcycle)
  • Zero BAC and drug level while on motorcycle
  • CANNOT supervise another learner rider
  • Fewer than 8 demerit points
  • No nighttime restriction (only Class 7 has this)
After Passing the Road Test
⚠ Critical Step — Do Not Forget

You must go to a registry agent office and pay a fee to re-class your licence. Passing the road test result alone does NOT change your licence class.

Class 7 who passed Class 6 road test

Receives a Class 6-GDL licence. Stays in 2-year probationary stage. Must complete an advanced road test to fully exit the GDL program.

Knowledge Test
  • 30 questions — rules, signs, safety, licensing
  • Based on the Rider's Guide AND the Driver's Guide
  • Taken at most Alberta registry agent offices on a computer
  • Must purchase a test permit for EACH attempt
  • Must PASS the knowledge test before the road test
Road Test
  • Approximately 60 minutes long
  • You must provide your own motorcycle with valid insurance and registration
  • Examiner follows you in a vehicle and communicates via one-way radio
  • 3 routine curb stops during the test — regardless of errors
  • If motorcycle is not mechanically safe, examiner will NOT conduct the test
  • Includes: equipment check, turns, intersections, balance, road position, speed control

Key Helmet Rules

Lighter colour = more visible to others. Try on for 5–10 min before buying. If removable without loosening chin strap = wrong fit or wrong size. Replace after any collision or hard impact. Never buy used. Never paint, alter, or use solvents on a helmet.

Eye & Face Protection
  • Must be motorcycle-specific, scratch-free, shatter-resistant, and secured firmly
  • Must give a clear view both forward and side-to-side
  • Must be large enough to fit over corrective glasses or sunglasses
  • TINTED protection must NOT be worn at night or in poor light — use clear shield only
  • A motorcycle windshield alone does NOT provide adequate face/eye protection

SystemWhat to Check
TiresAir pressure (match owner's manual), tread depth and wear, no objects in tread, no punctures, no cuts/bulges/cracks in sidewalls
Wheels/RimsWire spokes tight and complete, no cracks in rims, bearings lubricated, seals not leaking
LightsHeadlight (high AND low beam — must come on with engine running), tail light, brake light (activates with BOTH hand AND foot brake), all turn signals, reflectors clean and secure
FluidsOil level and colour (yellow = good; dark/black = needs changing), coolant level and hoses, fuel amount, brake fluid level, differential fluid. Check for leaks on the ground.
BrakesFront and rear tested separately — motorcycle should not move when fully applied
ClutchCable moves freely and feels tight; hydraulic clutch fluid at proper level
ThrottleMoves freely and returns to closed position WITHOUT assistance
Drive SystemChain: lubrication and proper tension; Belt: wear and proper tension; Shaft: no oil leaks
Battery & ElectricalBattery secure, terminals clean; horn works; engine cut-off switch stops engine; neutral light (green) works when in neutral; instrument lights work with key ON; hazard lights (if equipped) work; safety interlock switch on sidestand (if equipped)
MirrorsClean, properly adjusted (show edge of arm/shoulder), securely fastened
StandsSidestand and centre stand: no cracks or bends, retract and stay securely in place
Licence PlateMounted to rear fender, securely attached, clearly visible. Light working (if equipped).
Mirror Adjustment
Properly adjusted = shows

The edge of your arm or shoulder in each mirror, plus as much of the lane behind and adjacent lanes as possible. Objects may be CLOSER than they appear. Never rely entirely on mirrors — always shoulder check when changing lanes.

Starting Sequence
  • Release anti-theft lock (allows handlebars to steer freely)
  • Retract sidestand — ensure footing is secure
  • Adjust mirrors to show edge of your arm/shoulder
  • Ensure motorcycle is in NEUTRAL
  • Turn fuel switch to ON (if equipped)
  • Set engine cut-off switch to RUN
  • Open choke OR wait for fuel injection light to go OFF
  • Press starter until engine starts, then release
  • Warm engine, then slowly close choke (if equipped)
  • Ensure lights are on
  • Check mirrors, then shoulder check blind spot before moving
Controls by Hand/Foot HOT
ControlHand/FootFunction & Key Rule
ThrottleRight handEngine power. Wrist bent slightly DOWN, no higher than flat — prevents over-throttle.
Front brakeRight handFront wheel braking — most stopping power
Clutch leverLeft handEngage/disengage engine power to rear wheel. Squeeze = disengaged.
Gear shift leverLeft footUpshift: foot UNDER lever (one gear at a time only). Downshift: foot ON TOP of lever (multiple gears allowed).
Rear brakeRight footRear wheel braking
Riding Position
  • Arms: slightly bent and relaxed — not tense, not locked straight
  • Right wrist: slightly bent DOWN — prevents accidentally using too much throttle
  • Left hand: light but firm grip for easy clutch lever and switch access
  • Knees: pressed lightly against motorcycle to help maintain balance
  • Feet: FIRMLY on foot pegs AT ALL TIMES while moving — do not drag or point down
  • Eyes: looking WELL AHEAD in the direction of travel — never look down
Friction Zone
Definition — Know This

The point during clutch release where engine power FIRST begins to transfer to the rear wheel. Must coordinate slow clutch release + gentle throttle application for a smooth start without stalling.

Gear Shifting Rules HOT
ActionRule
UpshiftingONE gear at a time only. Foot UNDER lever. Roll throttle off, squeeze clutch, lift, release clutch + small throttle.
DownshiftingCan do MULTIPLE gears at once. Foot ON TOP of lever. Reduce speed to match gear before releasing clutch.
Downshift too fast for speedRear wheel may SKID
Curves/cornersReduce speed and gear BEFORE entering — not during the curve
Too much throttleLoss of control
Too little throttleEngine stalls
Uphill Starting
  • Use the front brake (or rear brake) to hold the bike in place initially
  • In first gear, reach friction zone, then gradually release brake while adding throttle
  • If clutch is released too quickly: engine may stall OR front wheel may lift off the road
  • Once moving smoothly and clutch fully released: remove fingers from clutch lever, return to hand grip

Lane Change Safe Sequence
  • 1. Check location is safe and legal
  • 2. Check ahead and in mirrors
  • 3. Shoulder check (clears blind spot)
  • 4. Signal
  • 5. Shoulder check AGAIN
  • 6. Move only if safe
⚠ Blind spots: If you cannot see the driver in their mirror, the driver likely cannot see YOU either. Always assume other drivers may NOT see you — ride proactively.
Space Cushion
  • The distance between yourself and all surrounding vehicles — fully controlled by YOU
  • Gives time and space to avoid a hazard
  • Maintain cushion to sides: avoid riding beside vehicles or in their blind spots
  • Do NOT ride beside another motorcycle in the same lane (unsafe AND illegal)
  • Plan ahead — minimize lane changes and only change when necessary

Signs a Parked Car May Pull Out
Watch For

Driver seated in the vehicle, signal lights flashing, brake lights lit, exhaust smoke visible, arm signal.

Distracted Riding — Traffic Safety Act Fines
  • Hand-held cell phone — even hands-free is a distraction; must pull over to use
  • Texting or emailing while riding
  • Electronic devices (laptops, video games, cameras, GPS entry)
  • Reading, writing, sketching
  • Personal grooming
Emergency Braking — Threshold Braking HOT
Threshold Braking =

Braking to the point JUST BEFORE the wheels lock. Maximum stopping force without losing steering control. Use BOTH front and rear brakes simultaneously and firmly.

Brake + Swerve Rules HOT
  • NEVER brake and swerve simultaneously — causes loss of control
  • Correct sequence: brake FIRST while upright → release brakes → swerve → reapply brakes after straightening
  • Keep feet on foot pegs during emergency braking (rear brake and gear changes still needed)
  • To swerve RIGHT: push on the RIGHT hand grip (counter-steering)
Locked Wheels — Exact Responses HOT
SituationCorrect Action
Rear wheel locks on paved surfaceIMMEDIATELY release the rear brake
Rear wheel locks on loose surface (gravel/sand)May keep it locked — releasing could cause worse instability
Front wheel locksRelease hand brake lever just enough to unlock wheel, then reapply gradually
Braking while front wheel is turnedApply brakes gradually with smooth threshold braking and less pressure than normal
Weight Transfer When Braking
Physics — Exam Question

When braking, the weight of the rider and cargo shifts FORWARD, increasing braking force on the FRONT wheel. This is why the front brake provides most of the stopping power.

Specific Emergency Situations
EmergencyWhat To Do
Throttle stuck openSqueeze CLUTCH lever AND use ENGINE KILL SWITCH simultaneously
ABS-equipped motorcycleApply firm continuous pressure — do NOT pump the brakes. Practice to get the feel.
Unavoidable object in pathEase off throttle and threshold brake BEFORE reaching it, shift weight back, stand slightly on pegs, grip firmly with relaxed arms
After riding over hard objectPull over when safe, stop, inspect tires and rims for damage
Motorcycle wobbleGrip handlebars firmly, close throttle GRADUALLY to slow down, pull off road. Do NOT brake suddenly.
Mirror habitCheck mirrors frequently to know what is behind you for safe emergency stops
Challenging Conditions
ConditionKey Response
Night / low visibilityReduce speed, increase following distance, use CLEAR eye protection only (not tinted)
Rain / wet roadsGreatly increases stopping distance. Brake earlier and more gently. Painted lines and metal surfaces are especially slippery when wet.
Gravel / loose surfacesSlow down before entering, ride straight and steady, avoid hard braking
Railroad tracksCross at 45–90 degree angle to prevent tire from getting caught in the groove
Before Adding a Passenger KEY
  • Only carry a passenger when you are an EXPERIENCED rider — not as a beginner
  • Motorcycle must have a seat designed to carry an extra person
  • Must have passenger hand grips AND foot rests installed
  • Check and adjust tire pressure for the extra weight
  • Adjust suspension/shock absorbers for extra weight
  • Check drive chain slack
  • Take the motorcycle OFF the side stand BEFORE the passenger mounts
Passenger Requirements — By Law
  • Must wear an APPROVED HELMET (legally mandatory)
  • Should wear full protective gear: helmet, protective clothing, proper footwear, eye protection
  • Feet must stay on foot pegs AT ALL TIMES — even when stopped (hot exhaust pipes and mufflers)
  • Must confirm with operator BEFORE getting on or off
How a Passenger Affects Riding HOT
EffectRequired Adjustment
Stopping distance increasesReduce speed EARLIER; increase following distance to 3–4 seconds
Slower accelerationLook for LARGER gaps in traffic when entering or merging
Extra weight in cornersRide at slower speeds on corners, curves, and bumps
Braking takes longerBrake earlier than normal in all situations
Instructions for the Passenger
  • Sit on the designed passenger seat ONLY — not on fuel tank, handlebars, or rear fender
  • Sit as far FORWARD as possible without crowding the operator
  • Hold operator's WAIST (recommended) or use passenger hand grips
  • Sit STILL — especially while manoeuvring at low speed
  • In turns, look over operator's shoulder in the direction of the turn
  • Move WITH the operator during acceleration and braking
  • Lean as the operator leans in turns — do NOT lean opposite
⚠ Avoid sudden acceleration with a passenger — they can lose balance and come off the motorcycle. Warn the passenger before any quick movements (lane changes, acceleration, etc.).
Carrying Cargo
  • Best method: saddle bags or tank bags designed for motorcycles
  • Load saddle bags with approximately equal weight on each side
  • Use elastic bungee cords or nets — check the load regularly during the trip
  • Do NOT attach cargo to handlebars or front fender
  • Do NOT allow a passenger to hold cargo in their hands
First Time Carrying a Passenger
Start Here

Begin at slow speeds in a traffic-free area, then progress to light traffic before riding in normal conditions. Communication and cooperation are extremely important to reduce injury risk.
